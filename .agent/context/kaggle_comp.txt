Overview
Description
Every minute counts in the emergency department. Across the world, emergency physicians and nurses make rapid, high-stakes triage decisions under extreme cognitive load, with incomplete information, and in environments that are chronically understaffed. Errors in triage severity scoring are not abstractions: they lead to delayed care, adverse outcomes, and preventable deaths.

The Laitinen-Fredriksson Foundation is a Finnish medical research foundation dedicated to advancing clinical decision support in acute and emergency medicine. Through Triagegeist, we invite data scientists, clinicians, and AI practitioners to tackle one of the most consequential problems in modern healthcare: can AI meaningfully support triage decisions in the emergency department?

The Challenge
Build an AI-powered tool or analytical system that assists clinicians in emergency triage. Your solution must address a specific, well-defined problem within the triage workflow. Examples include but are not limited to:

A model that predicts triage acuity level (e.g. ESI, MTS, or equivalent) from structured patient intake data
A natural language processing system that extracts and interprets chief complaint text to flag high-risk presentations
A decision support interface that surfaces deterioration risk for patients already in the waiting room
An analytical notebook that surfaces systematic triage bias patterns in a provided or public dataset
You are not required to build a full clinical application. A rigorous, well-documented Kaggle Notebook with a working proof-of-concept model and a thorough writeup is a complete and valid submission.

What You Will Build
Each team must submit:

A Kaggle Notebook containing your model, analysis, or prototype. The notebook must run end-to-end without errors and be made public at submission time.
A Project Writeup on Kaggle describing your approach, clinical motivation, methodology, and findings.

Why This Matters
Emergency triage systems in use today were designed decades ago and rely almost entirely on unaided human judgment. AI has the potential to reduce variability, flag overlooked risk, and extend the capacity of overburdened clinical teams. The Laitinen-Fredriksson Foundation will use insights from this competition to inform ongoing research programs and potential clinical pilot studies in partnership with hospital networks in Northern Europe.

Description
The Problem
Every minute counts in the emergency department. Triage nurses and physicians make rapid, high-stakes severity assessments under extreme cognitive load, with incomplete information, and in environments that are chronically understaffed. Errors in triage scoring are not abstractions: they lead to delayed care, adverse outcomes, and preventable deaths.

Established triage systems such as the Emergency Severity Index (ESI) and the Manchester Triage System (MTS) were designed decades ago and rely almost entirely on unaided human judgment. Inter-rater variability is well-documented. Systematic undertriage of certain patient populations is an active patient safety concern in the literature.

Can AI meaningfully support triage decisions in the emergency department? That is the question Triagegeist is built to answer.

The Challenge
Build an AI-powered tool or analytical system that assists clinicians in emergency triage. Your solution must address a specific, well-defined problem within the triage workflow.

Valid approaches include but are not limited to:

A model that predicts triage acuity level from structured patient intake data (vitals, demographics, chief complaint)
An NLP pipeline that extracts and interprets free-text chief complaint to flag high-risk presentations
A decision support system that surfaces deterioration risk for patients already waiting
A rigorous analytical notebook that identifies systematic triage bias patterns in a public or provided dataset
You are not required to build a full clinical application. A well-documented Kaggle Notebook with a working proof-of-concept and a thorough writeup is a complete and valid submission.

Data
Participants are free to use any publicly available dataset relevant to emergency triage and acute care. Recommended starting points include:

MIMIC-IV-ED — Emergency department data from Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Center, including triage notes, vitals, acuity scores, and outcomes. Available at physionet.org (credentialed access required).
NHAMCS — National Hospital Ambulatory Medical Care Survey, publicly available from the CDC, covering ED visits across the United States.
CTG / UCI datasets — Various physiological signal datasets available via the UCI Machine Learning Repository.
All datasets used must be cited and described in your notebook. You must confirm that your use of any dataset complies with its terms of access.
